## How to Actually Choose a Website Design Company in Chicago

Now here's the thing about agency selection: most businesses get it backwards.

They start by looking at portfolios, then price, then maybe check a few reviews. But the portfolio tells you almost nothing about whether they're right for *your* project. Here's the framework that actually works.

### Start With Your Actual Business Goals

Before you contact a single agency, get clear on what you're trying to accomplish. Not "I need a new website"—that's a tactic, not a goal.

Ask yourself:
- What's broken about the current site? (Low conversions? Hard to update? Looks dated? Not ranking?)
- What does success look like 6 months after launch? (More leads? Higher average order value? Better brand perception?)
- What's the real business impact you need? (10% revenue increase? 50 new leads per month? Enter a new market?)

The agency that's right for you depends entirely on these answers. If you need lead generation, Straight North or Comrade makes sense. If you're rebranding, Ruckus or Digital Silk. If you just need a solid, custom WordPress site that converts, Mojo Media or Lounge Lizard.

### Match Agency Specialization to Your Industry

Here's where people get it wrong: they assume good designers can design for anyone. That's technically true, but industry expertise matters enormously.

An agency that's built 20 law firm websites understands:
- What law clients look for (credentials, case results, trust signals)
- Compliance requirements (attorney advertising rules, accessibility)
- Common integrations (case management systems, intake forms)
- Conversion patterns (consultation requests vs. immediate calls)

Picture this for a second: you're hiring an agency that's never worked in your industry. They'll spend the first 2-3 weeks learning what you already know—on your dime. An experienced agency starts with that knowledge and focuses on execution.

When evaluating agencies, ask: "How many projects have you completed in [your industry] in the last 12 months?" If the answer is zero, they're learning on your project.

### Evaluate Their Process, Not Their Portfolio

The portfolio shows you what they've done for other people. The process tells you what working with them will actually feel like.

Ask these specific questions:
- **"Walk me through your typical project from kickoff to launch."** (Vague answers = undefined process)
- **"How many revision rounds are included?"** (Unlimited? Two? This reveals flexibility)
- **"What do you need from me, and when?"** (Shows whether they respect your time or expect 24/7 availability)
- **"What happens if we miss a deadline on our end?"** (Reveals how they handle real-world delays)
- **"Who owns the website files after launch?"** (You should own everything—code, design files, content)

If they can't clearly articulate their process, that's a red flag. You'll spend the entire project confused about what's happening and when.

### Understand Total Cost of Ownership

The website design fee is just the beginning. Here's what most people don't budget for:

**Ongoing costs after launch:**
- Hosting: $20-$500/month (depending on traffic and requirements)
- Maintenance: $100-$500/month (updates, backups, security monitoring)
- Content updates: $75-$150/hour (if you can't do it yourself)
- SEO: $500-$5,000+/month (if you want organic traffic growth)
- Emergency support: $100-$200/hour (when something breaks)

So here's what you actually do: Ask agencies about post-launch support. Do they offer maintenance packages? What's the response time if the site goes down? Can you update content yourself, or do you need them for every tiny change?

A $10K website that requires $500/month in updates becomes more expensive than a $15K site you can manage yourself.

### Test Responsiveness Before You Sign

This is the single best predictor of what working together will feel like: how they handle the sales process.

- Do they respond to emails within 24 hours?
- Do they ask insightful questions about your business, or just pitch their services?
- Do they show up on time for calls?
- Do they provide a detailed proposal, or just a generic PDF?

If they're slow, vague, or unresponsive during the courtship phase when they're *trying* to win your business, imagine how they'll behave once they have your money.

## Red Flags That Should Send You Running

Not every agency deserves your business. Here are the warning signs that should end the conversation immediately.

### They Promise #1 Google Rankings

No legitimate agency guarantees #1 rankings. Google's algorithm includes hundreds of factors, many outside anyone's control (competitor actions, algorithm updates, your domain age, your industry).

If an agency promises "first page in 30 days" or "#1 for your keyword," they're either lying or using black-hat tactics that'll get you penalized.

**What to look for instead:** Agencies that talk about sustainable SEO strategy, realistic timelines (6-12 months for competitive keywords), and focus on qualified traffic, not just rankings.

### They Use Templates But Don't Tell You

There's nothing inherently wrong with templates—they're faster and cheaper. The problem is agencies that charge custom prices for template work.

Ask directly: "Is this a custom design, or are you starting with a template?" If they dodge the question or say "semi-custom" (aka template with your colors), decide if that's acceptable. Just don't pay custom prices for template work.

**What to look for instead:** Transparent agencies that clearly explain their approach and price accordingly.

### They Don't Ask About Your Business

If an agency jumps straight to talking about their services without asking about your goals, your customers, your competitive landscape, or what's working/not working now—they're order-takers, not strategic partners.

Good agencies ask hard questions:
- "Who's your ideal customer, and what do they care about?"
- "What differentiates you from competitors?"
- "What's the main action you want visitors to take?"
- "What content/assets do you have already?"

**What to look for instead:** Agencies that spend more time asking questions than talking about themselves.

### Everything's Extra

Watch out for agencies with low base prices but nickel-and-dime you for everything else.

"$3,000 for the website... oh, but mobile optimization is +$800, SEO setup is +$1,200, contact form is +$300, SSL certificate is +$150..."

Suddenly that $3K site costs $8K, and you feel trapped.

**What to look for instead:** Fixed-price proposals that include everything you actually need, or transparent hourly rates with clear scope definitions.

### They Don't Explain Anything

Technical jargon is fine if they also explain it in plain English. But agencies that hide behind buzzwords ("We use AI-driven UX paradigms with blockchain-enabled CMS architecture") without explaining what that means are either incompetent or trying to confuse you into compliance.

**What to look for instead:** Agencies that explain technical decisions in terms of business impact, not just technology for technology's sake.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does it take to build a custom website in Chicago?

Most custom website projects take 6-12 weeks from kickoff to launch, depending on complexity. Here's the typical breakdown:

- **Planning/Strategy:** 1-2 weeks (discovery, wireframes, content planning)
- **Design:** 2-3 weeks (mockups, revisions, approval)
- **Development:** 2-4 weeks (building, testing, content population)
- **Revisions/Launch:** 1-2 weeks (final tweaks, training, go-live)

Enterprise projects (custom platforms, complex integrations) can take 3-8 months. Template-based sites can be done in 2-4 weeks.

The biggest variable? How quickly *you* provide feedback and content. Agencies can't design without your input or develop without your content.

### Should I hire a Chicago-based agency or work remotely?

Location matters less than it used to, but there are still advantages to local agencies:

**Pros of local Chicago agencies:**
- Face-to-face meetings if you prefer in-person collaboration
- Same timezone (no waiting 12 hours for responses)
- Understanding of local Chicago market, competition, culture
- Easier to verify credentials and visit their office

**When remote works fine:**
- You're comfortable with video calls and asynchronous communication
- The agency has proven remote processes
- You're looking for specialized expertise not available locally

For most small to mid-sized Chicago businesses, local makes sense. For enterprise or highly specialized needs, cast a wider net.

### What's the difference between web design and web development?

**Web Design** = How it looks and feels (visual design, user experience, layout, branding).

**Web Development** = How it works (coding, functionality, database, integrations).

Most projects need both. A designer creates mockups; a developer builds them into a functioning website. Many agencies (including all on this list) offer both services integrated.

If you hire separately, expect coordination headaches and finger-pointing when things don't work.

### Do I need WordPress, or are there better options?

WordPress powers 43% of all websites for good reasons:
- Huge ecosystem of plugins and themes
- Easy to find developers for updates
- You can manage content yourself without coding
- Flexible enough for simple sites or complex applications
- SEO-friendly out of the box

**Alternatives worth considering:**
- **Webflow:** Modern, fast, great visual editor (but smaller ecosystem)
- **WooCommerce:** If you're primarily e-commerce (WordPress-based e-commerce solution)
- **Custom platform:** If you have truly unique requirements WordPress can't handle

For most small to mid-sized businesses in Chicago, WordPress with custom design (not templates) is the sweet spot of flexibility, cost, and ease of management.

### How much should I budget for ongoing website maintenance?

Plan for $100-$500/month depending on your needs:

**Basic maintenance ($100-$200/month):**
- WordPress core and plugin updates
- Security monitoring
- Regular backups
- Uptime monitoring
- Emergency support if site goes down

**Standard maintenance ($200-$400/month):**
- Everything above, plus:
- Monthly content updates (text changes, new images)
- Performance optimization
- Quarterly SEO checkups
- Analytics reporting

**Comprehensive ($400-$500+/month):**
- Everything above, plus:
- Ongoing SEO and content strategy
- Conversion rate optimization
- Regular design refreshes
- Priority support

Some agencies include the first 30-60 days free, then transition to a maintenance plan. Others charge from day one.

### Can I update the website myself, or do I need the agency for every change?

This depends entirely on the platform and how the agency builds it.

**You CAN easily update yourself (no agency needed):**
- Adding blog posts
- Changing text on existing pages
- Swapping images
- Updating contact information or hours
- Adding new team members or products

**You'll likely NEED the agency for:**
- Adding entirely new page layouts
- Changing design elements (colors, fonts, layouts)
- Adding new functionality or integrations
- Technical troubleshooting or bug fixes

Good agencies build sites with client editing in mind—using page builders (like Elementor) or clean WordPress interfaces. Bad agencies build sites that require developer access for every tiny change (which keeps you dependent on them).

Ask during the selection process: "Show me how I'd update a page myself." If they can't easily demonstrate it, that's a red flag.

### What should I prepare before contacting a web design agency?

Having these ready speeds up the process and gets you more accurate quotes:

**Essential:**
- Clear business goals (what you want the site to accomplish)
- Target audience description (who you're trying to reach)
- Budget range (agencies tailor proposals to budget)
- Timeline/deadline (if you have one)

**Very helpful:**

- Competitor sites you admire (shows aesthetic preferences)

- Examples of sites you like (not necessarily competitors)

**Nice to have:**
- Brand guidelines (logos, fonts, colors)
- Photography/imagery assets
- Existing analytics data (traffic, conversions)

You don't need everything perfect—agencies can help—but the more clarity you provide upfront, the better the proposal and timeline.

### How do I know if an agency's portfolio work is actually theirs?

Good question. Some agencies showcase work done by freelancers, previous employees, or white-label partners.

**What to ask:**
- "Which team members worked on this project?" (Confirms current team involvement)
- "Can I speak with this client as a reference?" (Verifies the relationship)
- "What was your specific role in this project?" (Design only? Development too? Strategy?)

**Red flags:**
- Portfolio work doesn't match their stated services
- They can't provide client references
- Work looks drastically different in style (suggests multiple freelancers)
- They dodge questions about their process on portfolio pieces

Reputable agencies are transparent about their work and happy to connect you with past clients.

### Should I sign a long-term contract, or keep it project-based?

For the initial website build, project-based makes sense. You shouldn't be locked into a long-term relationship before you've seen their work quality and communication style.

**Project-based contracts should include:**
- Fixed deliverables and milestones
- Clear payment schedule (typically 1/3 upfront, 1/3 mid-project, 1/3 at launch)
- Ownership rights (you own all code, design, and content)
- Termination clause (what happens if you need to end early)

**Long-term contracts (monthly retainers) make sense for:**
- Ongoing SEO and content marketing
- Regular site updates and maintenance
- Continuous optimization and testing

Start with the project. If they deliver and you're happy, *then* consider ongoing services.

### What happens if I'm not happy with the design?

This is why the agency's revision policy matters enormously.

**Questions to ask before signing:**
- "How many design revisions are included?"
- "What happens if I want changes beyond that?"
- "At what point is design considered 'approved' and locked?"

**Good revision policies:**
- 2-3 rounds of revisions included (sometimes unlimited)
- Clear feedback process (you provide consolidated feedback, not scattered emails)
- Specific revision window (you have 5 business days to review, for example)

**Red flags:**
- Only one revision round included
- Vague answers about revision process
- Extra revisions cost 50-100% of original design fee

The best protection: review their portfolio thoroughly upfront. If you love their previous work, you'll likely love what they create for you.
