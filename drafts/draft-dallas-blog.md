H2: What to Look for When Choosing a Dallas Web Design Company

Now here's what most people get wrong when selecting a design agency.

They focus on portfolios. And yes, portfolios matter—you should like the aesthetic and see evidence the agency can execute. But portfolios don't tell you whether the agency is right for you.

Here's what actually matters.

**Industry Experience (More Than You Think)**

Agencies that specialize in your industry understand your customers' buying journey, common objections, and what makes them convert. They know what competitors are doing and how to differentiate your brand.

A general agency can create a beautiful website. A specialist can create a website that generates leads because they understand your market.

If you're a healthcare practice, work with an agency that's built medical websites. If you're a contractor, find someone with home services experience. The learning curve is shorter, and results come faster.

**Process Transparency (Red Flag Test)**

Ask the agency to walk you through their process. If they can't clearly explain the steps from kickoff to launch—or if their answer is vague—walk away.

Good agencies have documented processes. They know how many revisions are included, what deliverables you'll receive, when milestones happen, and how communication works.

If an agency says "we'll figure it out as we go," you're about to enter project chaos.

**Pricing Structure (Fixed vs. Hourly)**

Fixed pricing gives you cost certainty. You know the investment upfront, and the agency assumes the risk of scope creep.

Hourly billing protects the agency but creates uncertainty for you. Invoices can spiral, timelines extend, and you're constantly monitoring hours.

For most small to mid-sized business websites, fixed pricing is the better option. For complex, evolving projects where scope isn't clear, hourly might make sense.

Ask upfront: How do you price projects? What's included? What triggers additional costs?

**Ownership & Access (Non-Negotiable)**

You should own your website, domain, hosting, and all assets when the project ends. This sounds obvious, but some agencies maintain control to create dependency.

Ask explicitly: Will I own the website? Will I have full admin access? Can I move the site to another host if needed? If the answer to any of these is no, that's a dealbreaker.

**Ongoing Support (But Not Lock-In)**

Websites require maintenance—software updates, security patches, backups, occasional fixes. Some agencies include this for a period. Others charge monthly fees.

Ongoing support is valuable, but you shouldn't be locked in. Make sure you can cancel anytime or manage maintenance yourself if you choose.

The best arrangement: optional support packages you can start or stop as needed.

**Results Tracking (How They Define Success)**

Ask the agency: How do you measure success? What metrics do you track?

If they talk only about design awards, aesthetics, or "modern feel," that's a red flag. Good agencies talk about traffic, conversion rates, lead generation, bounce rates, and business outcomes.

You're not paying for a pretty website. You're paying for a business asset that generates returns.

**Communication Style (Gut Check)**

Pay attention to responsiveness during the sales process. If it takes three days to get a reply before you've signed a contract, imagine how communication will be during the project.

Good agencies respond promptly, answer questions directly, and make you feel like a priority. If you're getting bad vibes early, trust your instinct.

H2: Custom WordPress vs. Template Sites (What Actually Matters)

This debate comes up constantly in web design conversations. Should you go custom or use a template?

Here's the honest answer: it depends on your goals, budget, and how you plan to use the website.

**Template Sites (Wix, Squarespace, Pre-Made WordPress Themes)**

Templates are pre-built website designs you customize with your content, colors, and images. They're faster and cheaper because the structure already exists.

**Pros:**
- Significantly lower cost ($1,500-$5,000 typical)
- Faster launch (2-4 weeks)
- Easy to update yourself
- Thousands of options available

**Cons:**
- Limited customization (you're constrained by template structure)
- Looks similar to other sites using the same template
- Harder to optimize for conversions (layouts aren't built for your specific customer journey)
- Can have bloated code (affects page speed, SEO)
- May hit limitations as your business grows

**Custom WordPress Sites**

Custom builds start from scratch. Designers create layouts specifically for your brand, content, and conversion goals using page builders like Elementor or custom code.

**Pros:**
- Unique design that matches your brand exactly
- Optimized specifically for your customer journey
- Conversion-focused layouts (not generic structures)
- Cleaner code (better performance and SEO)
- Scalable as your business grows
- Full control over every element

**Cons:**
- Higher cost ($8,000-$30,000+ typical)
- Longer timeline (6-12 weeks)
- Requires experienced designer/developer

**So Which Should You Choose?**

Go with a template if:
- Your budget is under $5K
- You need something online fast (under 4 weeks)
- Your business is very early stage
- The website is primarily informational (not driving revenue)
- You're comfortable with DIY updates

Go custom if:
- Your website is a primary revenue driver
- You need specific functionality or integrations
- Your brand needs to stand out from competitors
- You want conversion-optimized layouts
- You have budget ($8K+) and time (6+ weeks)
- SEO and performance are critical

Here's the thing most designers won't tell you.

Templates have gotten really good. Modern WordPress themes and platforms like Webflow offer extensive customization. For many small businesses, a well-executed template site does the job perfectly fine.

But if your website is a core business asset—if it generates leads, drives sales, or significantly impacts revenue—custom is worth the investment. The ROI justifies the cost.

The worst choice is picking custom design when you actually need a template (overspending for your stage), or picking a template when you need custom (underinvesting when the website matters).

Be honest about where your business is and what you need. Then choose accordingly.
